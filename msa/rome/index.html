AnalysisJune 2026 · Generated by Economic Impact Group, LLC from the data shown on this page

Every claim in this section is derived from a specific indicator displayed elsewhere on this page; section names in parentheses point to the source. This narrative is generated deterministically from the live data refresh shown in the as-of stamp — figures update on each refresh.

Rome's unemployment rate is 3.3% (Mar 2026) — the 6th-lowest of Georgia's 14 metros — yet total nonfarm employment is essentially flat year-over-year (−0.4%, Current Employment Trends). The movement is sectoral — retail trade (+2.0%) and education & health (+0.9%) are still adding jobs, while government (−1.9%) and manufacturing (−1.6%) are contracting (Industry Employment).

The metro's base is tilted toward manufacturing: manufacturing is 13.0% of employment versus 8.6% nationally (Comparative Employment) — location quotients of 1.5, the metro's defining industries (Economic Drivers). The largest sectors by headcount are education & health (24.0%) and manufacturing (13.0%). On pay, professional-services pay averages $19,708 a year against $101,525 nationally while manufacturing averages $71,864 (QCEW, where disclosed).

Goods exports totaled $0.1B in 2024 (Exports), about 2% of gross metro product — a modest trade intensity. USMCA is the largest single destination market.

House-price growth has cooled to +3.7% YoY (House Price Index). The EIG valuation model places Rome on its income-implied fair value (-0% deviation), so prices track local income fundamentals rather than running ahead of them. Builders authorized roughly 330 single-family and 0 multifamily permits in 2025 (headline table). Affordability sits above breakeven: the EIG affordability index reads 136 (Housing Affordability) — a reading over 100 means the median household can qualify for the median home.

Population reached about 99,693 (2024) and grew 0.7% into the latest year (Geographic Profile), with net migration of +279 the main driver. The metro's age profile shows median age 38.2, younger than the US (~39), with Gen Z and Millennials a combined ~40% of residents (Population by Age). Educational attainment is below the US average, with 25.9% of adults holding a bachelor's degree or higher.

A Gini coefficient of 0.47 sits below the US average (~0.48), pointing to broadly middling income inequality (Inequality table). Within the metro, roughly a fifth of neighborhoods report a median household income under $50k; the poverty rate of about 16% runs above the US average; the industrial diversity score (0.86, Industrial Structure) is below the Georgia average.

Rome pairs real strengths — a 3.3% unemployment rate, positive in-migration, on-trend home valuations, an EIG credit score of A (stable outlook) — with employment growth slowing (−0.4% YoY).

Sections flagged with a Proxy pill use Economic Impact Group composite measures where the source data is not directly published (business cycle index, valuation model, vitality, quality-of-life). The Vitality composite blends labor-force participation (ACS B23025), latest-year personal-income growth (BEA), young-adult 25–34 share (ACS B01001) and net-migration rate (IRS SOI). The Quality-of-Life composite blends air quality (EPA median AQI), long commute share (ACS), housing affordability and poverty (ACS). Crime is deliberately excluded from the Quality-of-Life composite: no clean, automated, current MSA-level crime feed exists — the FBI Crime Data API reports only by agency/state and is key-gated, and the FBI's by-MSA crime table was discontinued after 2019, so rather than freeze a stale 2019 figure the metric is omitted. Forecast columns (ARIMA) and the EIG credit score are live composite estimates, labeled accordingly.

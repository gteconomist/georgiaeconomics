  • Population growth (1.6% YoY in 2025) runs roughly 3× the US pace.
  • Unemployment (3.1%) sits ~1 pt below the US rate and is the lowest of any GA MSA.
  • Manufacturing share (11.4% of jobs) is well above GA (8.6%) and US (8.1%).
  • Transportation & warehousing share (9.7% of jobs) is more than 2× the US rate.

  • Goods-movement concentration raises tariff & trade-policy exposure.

Labor Market

Savannah's unemployment rate held at 3.1% through the latest six months (Health Check), roughly one full point below the US rate and the lowest of Georgia's 14 MSAs. The labor force participation rate has risen from 63.4% in November to 63.8% in April — a 0.4 pt expansion that, combined with the falling unemployment rate, signals a tightening rather than weakening market. Total nonfarm employment is up 2.5% year-over-year as of April (Current Employment Trends), led by manufacturing (+7.1%), transportation & warehousing (+6.4%) and education & health (+4.1%). The diffusion index sits in the upper-60s — broad-based, not concentrated, expansion.

Sector Mix

The metro's industrial signature is goods movement and goods production. Transportation & utilities account for 9.7% of jobs versus 4.6% nationally (Comparative Employment table), and manufacturing accounts for 11.4% versus 8.1% nationally, with the bulk in durable goods. The flip side: information employment is just 1.1% of the metro (vs. 1.9% US), professional & business services 10.8% (vs. 14.3% US), and financial activities 4.2% (vs. 5.8% US). Average annual earnings in durable manufacturing reach $108,200 — well above the US figure of $92,000 — while earnings in professional & business services trail the national average by ~$26K per worker.

Trade Exposure

Exports total $9.9B, equivalent to 29.6% of GMP (Exports section), ranking Savannah 38th among 387 US metros for trade intensity. Transportation equipment ($4.8B) and chemicals ($1.3B) lead, and Asia ($3.8B) and the EU ($2.6B) are the two largest destinations. This mix raises tariff and global-demand sensitivity well above the typical US metro — one of the few clear structural weaknesses in the local picture.

Housing

FHFA house-price growth is moderating to 3.6% YoY (long indicators table) from a 17.8% peak in 2021. Supply has responded: single-family permits ran at 11.2 per 1,000 residents in 2025, the highest rate of any Georgia MSA. Affordability has still deteriorated meaningfully — the Housing Affordability index has fallen from a 2019 peak of 280 to 118 today, an indication that the median household just barely qualifies for the median home. The EIG valuation model places Savannah broadly in line with local income fundamentals: prices have stayed within roughly ±6% of their income-implied fair value across 2019–2024, so the affordability squeeze reflects rapid income-paced appreciation rather than a speculative premium.

Demographics & Migration

Population reached 410,000 in 2024 and grew 1.6% in 2025 — roughly 3× the US rate. Net migration has stepped up sharply: +5,700 in 2025 vs. +2,400 in 2024, the highest pace since 2019. Domestic migration is the larger of the two components and rose 116% YoY. The metro is younger than the US average (median age 36.2 vs. 38.5 national; Geographic Profile), with a higher share of Gen Z and Millennials (45% combined vs. 42% US). Educational attainment is comparable to the US average overall, but the metro skews toward "some college, no degree" rather than 4-year-and-above completion.

Inequality & Structural Position

Gini coefficient of 0.46 places Savannah at rank 187 of 411 metros — close to the US median (Inequality table). The block-group income ratio is more concerning at rank 78, indicating real geographic separation of income within the MSA. Poverty rate at 13.4% is slightly above the US average. The industrial diversity score (0.76) is above the GA average (0.71), so the metro is structurally less monocultural than its goods-movement tilt would suggest.

Synthesis

Savannah's near-term momentum is unambiguous: tightening labor market, broad-based job growth, record net migration, and continued manufacturing acceleration. Structural risks center on three areas: (1) trade-policy sensitivity given the export share, (2) housing affordability erosion despite responsive supply, and (3) underdevelopment in higher-paying white-collar sectors (information, finance, professional services) that elsewhere drive earnings convergence with the national average. Forecast figures are not published here pending the Phase 2 model rollout.

About this report. Prepared by Economic Impact Group, LLC and published on the Georgia Economics platform. Built from public & subscription-free data sources only: BLS (CES, QCEW, LAUS), BEA (Regional GDP & Personal Income), Census Bureau (PEP, ACS, BPS, Business Formation Statistics, USA Trade Online, F-33 School System Finances), FHFA (HPI), EPA AirData (annual AQI by CBSA), FRED, IRS SOI migration, ITA TradeStats, and Georgia Ports Authority monthly statistics. Sections flagged with a Proxy pill use Economic Impact Group composite measures where the source data is not directly published (business cycle index, valuation model, vitality, quality-of-life). The Vitality composite blends labor-force participation (ACS B23025), latest-year personal-income growth (BEA), young-adult 25–34 share (ACS B01001) and net-migration rate (IRS SOI). The Quality-of-Life composite blends air quality (EPA median AQI), long commute share (ACS), housing affordability and poverty (ACS), and enrollment-weighted per-pupil school spending (Census F-33, Chatham/Effingham/Bryan districts). Crime is deliberately excluded from the Quality-of-Life composite: no clean, automated, current MSA-level crime feed exists — the FBI Crime Data API reports only by agency/state and is key-gated, and the FBI's by-MSA crime table was discontinued after 2019, so rather than freeze a stale 2019 figure the metric is omitted. Forecast columns and credit-score values are marked as model-pending and will be populated in Phase 2 of the rollout.
